What do cats, group selfies, and limericks have to do with each other? Surprisingly, AI, as PETAL learned at the C \+ M 2026 AI Conference: The Human Future in the Age of AI.

We arrived at Hemmerdinger Hall bright and early (read: 8:30 am) to catch the opening sessions. Dean Michael A. Lindsey, of the Silver School of Social Work, and Georgina Dopico, NYU’s Provost, both spoke about encouraging AI integration across the university. Indeed, while NYU has had centers dedicated to [high-performance computing](https://www.nyu.edu/about/news-publications/news/2020/november/GreeneSupercomputer.html) and [research on responsible AI](https://airesponsibly.net/) since 2020, the university has recently been upping its investment in science and technology. Just last year, it [established the Courant Institute School of Mathematics, Computing, and Data Science](https://www.nyu.edu/about/news-publications/news/2025/november/nyu-establishes-new-school--the-courant-institute-school-of-math.html), formally separating the math department from the College of Arts and Science (CAS), linking the CAS and Tandon CS departments, and integrating the Center for Data Science. 

Both speakers also emphasized the importance of addressing human issues, with Dopico even quoting Gwendolyn Brooks:

>>*We are each other’s*  
*harvest:*  
*we are each other’s*  
*business:*  
*we are each other’s*  
*magnitude and bond.*

Indeed, this conference was broken up into sessions relating to higher education, social services, mental health, and the future of work (we were unfortunately only able to attend the first two). 

Following the opening remarks, Yann LeCun (NYU’s Courant Institute) engaged in a fireside chat with Ruopeng An (of NYU CDS). As they got up from their seats, so too did many audience members, to take photos of (we presume) LeCun. No wonder — LeCun won the Turing Award for his work on convolutional neural networks and was the chief A.I. scientist at Meta for over a decade before leaving to found his own company, [Advanced Machine Intelligence (AMI) Labs](https://amilabs.xyz/). 

The discussion started with LeCun’s famous cat metaphor. Today’s frontier models have [passed the bar](https://law.stanford.edu/2023/04/19/gpt-4-passes-the-bar-exam-what-that-means-for-artificial-intelligence-tools-in-the-legal-industry/) and [achieved gold-level scores in the International Mathematical Olympiad](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/21/technology/google-ai-international-mathematics-olympiad.html#:~:text=An%20artificial%20intelligence%20system%20built,competition%20for%20high%20school%20students.); they have [surpassed us](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2024-ai-index-report) in image classification and visual reasoning. Yet they [still have issues](https://jdsemrau.substack.com/p/the-importance-of-object-permanence) with object permanence and physical reasoning. They have around the same number of parameters as the average cat has synapses, but are far less capable when it comes to interacting with the real world. And that’s just cat-level intelligence: cats have a hundred times fewer neurons than a human brain. In fact, “a four-year old,” Dr. LeCun told the audience, “has seen as much data in the world as an LLM.” 

LeCunn’s point was that intelligence is not just the gathering of skills, as we often think of it — it might not even require language. Rather, he defined it as the ability to learn *new* skills and adapt to new situations. Moving on, LeCun and An covered the difference between reinforcement and supervised learning, and discussed LeCun’s interest in improving AI’s spatial awareness. LeCun’s company, AMI Labs, is trying to build world models that will use video and spatial data to simulate processes in the physical world. LeCun ended the talk by describing his vision of a global consortium that would develop an open source frontier model containing every language and value system. When pressed on how this would work, he argued that this model could be parameterized instead of continuously taking in new data, which would render the issue of training time and environmental costs moot. Per tradition, we took a group selfie at the end of the talk. 

The next panel, AI in Higher Education, featured An as well as Drs. Ben Glaser (Yale) and Stratos Efstathiadis (NYU). Glaser, an English professor, is also Yale’s director of AI initiatives in the humanities, working with faculty to incorporate AI into research and teaching. One of Glaser’s research focuses is computational poetics, or the application of technology like natural language processing to poetry. Taking an example from a [project that used NLP to detect limericks](https://aclanthology.org/2021.latechclfl-1.9.pdf), Glaser noted that the model wasn’t able to perfectly detect patterns in meter and rhyme. Indeed, echoing LeCunn, he writes in his paper:

> “A young child learns to rhyme, decide syllable breaks, and can develop a sense of poetic meter very quickly – say, in a single grade school class (if the plethora of teachers’ worksheets available on the internet is any indication). That a language model with billions of parameters and thousands of examples isn’t yet perfect says a lot about both the models we develop and the human process of learning as well.”

On the integration of AI tools into education ([NYU, for example, has partnered with Google Gemini](https://www.nyu.edu/faculty/teaching-and-learning-resources/Student-Learning-with-Generative-AI.html)), Glaser spoke about the gap between the university’s rush to supply these tools and the lower level of demand for them by faculty and students alike. Of course, being an English professor, Glaser was also asked for his thoughts on AI being used to write essays, and his opinion on the most important thing a student should do for themself. His response was simple: “fix a sentence.” Working on such a micro level, he argued, is important for grappling with what you want to *think* —- relying too much on tools which predict the ending of your sentences can erode your ability to think critically.   

Efstathiadis, NYU’s Senior Director of Research Technology Services, spoke about the university’s increasing involvement in computing research and membership in New York State’s [EmpireAI](https://www.nyu.edu/about/news-publications/news/2024/january/nyu-joins-gov--hochul-s--empire-ai--initiative-to-make-new-york-.html) initiative. Last year, NYU acquired over a million square feet of space which will be dedicated to classrooms, laboratories, and the [most powerful supercomputer in New York State](https://www.nyu.edu/about/news-publications/news/2025/october/nyu-unveils--torch---the-most-powerful-supercomputer-in-new-york.html), which Efstathiadis helps manage access to. Efstathiadis also discussed NYU’s partnership with KAIST and its joint venture, the [Global AI Frontier Lab](https://www.nyu.edu/research/research-centers-and-institutes/global-ai-frontier-lab.html) in Brooklyn. 

# Side Quests and Conversations

One of the best parts of attending conferences like this is being able to meet new people. For example, Vivian had a fascinating conversation with [Dr. Thomas Neubert](https://med.nyu.edu/faculty/thomas-a-neubert), a professor at NYU Grossman’s Department of Neuroscience, about the role of cybersecurity in hospitals. Human data, he said, is “very valuable,” as it is an inherent identifier. Unlike your browsing history, which is only tangentially tied to your identity, a marker like your blood type is immutable and intrinsic to you. Many hospitals, including NYU Langone, are facing an [increasing number](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2950386825000103) of cyberattacks, and are working to improve their cybersecurity protocols. 

Tarunya and Vivian also met Abigail Keegan, a master’s student from Pace University working in the [Pace AI Lab](https://www.pace.edu/seidenberg/faculty-and-research/centers-and-labs/pace-artificial-intelligence-lab). She and her lab work on many different applications of AI, one of the latest being Senegalese textiles. At the time, Keegan was specifically looking at ways to incorporate [OpenClaw](https://openclaw.ai/), a personal AI assistant, into the lab’s research.
